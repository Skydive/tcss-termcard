ABOUT
Trinity College Science Society (TCSS) is a student-run organisation aiming to promote science among students of the University of Cambridge as well as the general public. We are one of the most active science societies in Cambridge, hosting weekly talks with distinguished speakers along with the annual internship event and symposium.

Past speakers include Fields medal laureate Michael Atiyah, President of the Royal Society Venkataraman Ramakrishnan, and Nobel laureate Sir Paul Nurse.

To keep our commitment to promote the discussion, understanding, and enjoyment of science, we will try our best to provide the connection between our audience and the speakers.

All of the talk and refreshments are free with the generous support of Trinity College. Please come and enjoy the talks, ask questions, and make the most out of your experience! The committee will always be there to support you to get the most out of our events.

We sincerely hope we can see all of you to join us in another year of scientific adventure!

Communicating chemistry using infographics

Speech rhythm: a metaphor?

Department of Phonetics
A talk intending to reveal how languages differ in terms of 'rhythm' in a general sense and how we've tried to quantify this; a mention of how one matches words to music in different languages; and the possibility that speech is actually 'anti-rhythmic' in nature.

Thursday 1 November
Dangerous consequences of the decline of coral reefs
Operation Wallacea
opwall.com
The value of coral reefs to human populations and the multiple threats that are currently operating to reduce reef health. How coral reef researchers and conservationists are trying to reverse the fate of these incredibly important and fragile ecosystems.
Thursday 8 November
Interacting with instabilities: Nonlinearity in structural engineering
Professor Ahmer Wadee
Structural engineering relies fundamentally on mathematics. We shall discuss some recent research work demonstrating that nonlinear mathematics provides key tools to make informed decisions about whether instabilities are catastrophic or may be exploited to enhance structural performance.
Thursday 15 November
Exoplanets and the quest for universal life
Professor Didier Queloz
Besides the gas giants, current technology now allows us to discover Earth-like exoplanets, and for some of them, even the atmospheric properties are retrievable. Based on this early results, we will discuss on the possibilities of exoplanets to harbour life.
Thursday 22 November
Life and death in an old enzyme: the ATP synthase
Professor John Walker
While ATP synthases normally maintain a unique direction of rotation to synthesise ATP, the direction can be reversed as ATP is hydrolised. However, some eubacterial ATP synthases are unable to do this. Since the ATP synthase in Mycobacterium tuberculosis is a validated drug target, differences between human and pathogen ATP synthases can be exploited to cope antibiotic resistance.
Thursday 29 November
Unification and progress in modern theoretical physics
Professor Michael Green
The talk will be a non-technical overview of the evolution of Modern Physics, from Relativity and Quantum theory to QFT and String theory. These recent developments have potential applications to different areas of physics from what they were originally intended for.
